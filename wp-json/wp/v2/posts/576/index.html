The distinction between science, engineering, and technology is not always clear. Science<\/a> is systematic knowledge of the physical or material world gained through observation and experimentation.[17]<\/a><\/sup> Technologies are not usually exclusively products of science, because they have to satisfy requirements such as utility<\/a>, usability<\/a>, and safety<\/a>.[18]<\/a><\/sup><\/p>\n\n\n\n

Engineering is the goal-oriented<\/a> process of designing and making tools and systems to exploit natural phenomena for practical human means, often (but not always) using results and techniques from science. The development of technology may draw upon many fields of knowledge, including scientific, engineering, mathematical<\/a>, linguistic<\/a>, and historical<\/a> knowledge, to achieve some practical result.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Technology is often a consequence of science and engineering, although technology as a human activity precedes the two fields. For example, science might study the flow of electrons<\/a> in electrical conductors<\/a> by using already-existing tools and knowledge. This new-found knowledge may then be used by engineers to create new tools and machines such as semiconductors<\/a>, computers<\/a>, and other forms of advanced technology. In this sense, scientists and engineers may both be considered technologists<\/a>; the three fields are often considered as one for the purposes of research and reference<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
